Weighing In: The "Unreliable KD Scandal"

by Paulo C Camacho

May 1, 2014



You know, I have to admit, I hate these disposable stories in the middle of sports news cycles, sometimes. Sure, it’s something to talk about, but sometimes, it just gets obnoxious.


Case in point: a lot of sports hubs, including ESPN and TNT Sports, have been focusing on a headline in the Oklahoman, a newspaper serving the greater Oklahoma City area.


With their hometown Thunder down 3-2 to the 7-seeded Grizzlies, and facing elimination in Memphis (as of writing this article), the paper criticized their star, and most-likely MVP winner, for his play thus far in the series. He hasn’t been playing as well as he had been during the regular season, and they weren’t wrong in their article. But it was the headline that caught the most flak:



"Mr. Unreliable".

While it probably wasn’t the best idea to call one of the best players in the league something that suggested that he could not be relied upon to win games, it was the media cycle’s customary employ of the “Mountain out of Molehill” strategy. Especially when Durant’s teammates came to his aid, calling out the Oklahoman, and Durant’s own mother tweeting the following:



Honestly, people need to just calm down. It’s just a headline.

And one taken out of context, at that.

Much of the response against the Oklahoman (as far as I could tell) was that they were outraged that a surefire MVP, and one as reliable as Durant had been during the regular season, could ever be considered “unreliable”. The idea that anyone would say that is ridiculous.

Well, of course it’s ridiculous, if you consider Durant’s career of clutch shooting.

But, the fact was, the story was criticizing how a player like Durant, who has been so reliable throughout his career in the scoring department, could come up as short as he has to find his team down, with a do-or-die game at their feet. After all, it was Durant who missed the potential game-tying and game-winning free throws in Game 4 that put them in the hole in the first place – free throws that no one would ever question he would make.

This is also a guy who had a 50.3% field goal percentage in the regular season, and is shooting at 40.0% so far in the postseason. Not necessarily terrible, but still relatively worse than what is expected of an elite player like Durant.

Again, was it a good idea for the Thunder’s hometown paper to insinuate that Durant was an unreliable player in general? No. Obviously not.

At the same time, if people had taken the time to read the actual article – which the headline was meant to urge people do in the first place – instead of shooting at the hip at a ridiculous headline, they would understand that the article was meant to criticize Durant’s play so far in this first round series, and not his overall play. He’s essentially become unreliable in this playoff series bubble.

But, hey – go ahead and keep taking the headline out of context. You know, considering it’s a slow news day and all.
