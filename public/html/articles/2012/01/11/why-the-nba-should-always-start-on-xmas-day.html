Why The NBA Should ALWAYS Start on Christmas Day

by Paulo C Camacho

January 11, 2012




It’s been two weeks since the 2011-2012 NBA season began. The 66-game regular season, while exciting, should prove to be trying on a number of teams around the league. And, as basketball will soon become the main focus, as the NFL and college football begin to wind down, the NBA will heed the call with many of the marquis names – and teams – hunting for a playoff spot, and, ultimately, a championship.

But, the circumstances of the season’s late start has inadvertently brought up some interesting – if not hypothetical – possibilities for the future of the Association. After all, the recent NBA lockout brought on the abbreviated NBA season in the first place. And, after the players and owners kissed, made up, and signed a new collective bargaining agreement, the only question left was: When do they start?

Why not Christmas Day?



Considering the timing of the new agreement, it made perfect sense: Besides the presents and the parades, the NBA owns December 25, in the realm of American sport. With that in mind, league officials structured a 66-game season that they set to start on Christmas. And, what do you know: the ratings for the Christmas Day start were up by as much as 77%, compared to last year. Obviously, that mostly has to do with the publicity the NBA had with its lockout, but the numbers are still significant.

With that in mind, it did get me thinking: with the popularity of the NBA, especially on such a popular holiday like Christmas, why don’t they just start an 82-game regular season EVERY December 25?



Now, I realize that this year was obviously a special circumstance – the regular season simply could not start the regular season when it usually did – around the end of October – or, at least, that’s what they claimed at the time. So, why, you may ask, would the league – or should they – start the season nearly two months later?

Well, let’s think about it.

First of all, there’s the perception that the populace simply doesn’t care the NBA is going on until Christmas Day. The NBA on Christmas is such a huge deal in Americana simply because that’s naturally the best time to start caring about professional basketball: the NFL regular season is winding down, the MLB Hot Stove is technically in remission, and the NHL has proven in the past that it cannot take hold of the national imagination.

It’s hard not to notice that the NBA, most years, receives little to no huge fanfare in the public arena when it usually starts, around Halloween, anyway – sports fans are usually so engulfed in the World Series and the ongoing drama in the NFL and college football, it’s difficult for the NBA to come in and grab the populace’s attention. If the Association starts on Christmas, it has a pulpit to announce itself as having arrived, all their own.

So, lets take a look at an 82-game regular season, as it is currently set up, taking the template from the 2010 season:

October 26, 2010: Opening Day

February 18-20, 2011: All-Star Weekend

April 13, 2011: Last day of Regular Season

April 16 – May 26, 2011: Duration of the NBA Postseason

Keeping those four dates in mind, the duration of the NBA season actually clashes with many other major sports that spectators could easily pay more attention to. For example, the start date of the regular season – the last week of October – directly conflicts with a few major American sports occurrences, namely the middle of the NFL regular season; major college football matchups; and the lead-up to the NCAA basketball season. It’s not the most optimum start date to a professional season.

For that matter, the duration of the playoffs isn’t the best of times, either, as it also conflicts with other marquis sports. Think about it: April to June is associated more with the start of the MLB season, than anything in the NBA, which is actually kind of a shame. The end of the regular season isn’t given any favors, either – what should be considered the most exciting time of the regular season falls into the black hole of March Madness (February-early April).

Now, think about the prospect of a full NBA regular season that starts on Christmas Day – it would be a season that would begin two calendar months (60 days) later:

December 25, 2011: Opening Day

April 13-15, 2012: All-Star Weekend

June 11, 2012: Last day of Regular Season

June 14-July 24, 2012: Duration of NBA Postseason

Take another look at those dates: While the All-Star Break can be negotiable (so it doesn’t conflict with MLB Opening Day), it could create much more interest, just looking at the schedule from a seasonal standpoint. For example, the final weeks of the NBA regular season, in this hypothetical situation, would fall in the waning weeks of May, and the playoffs could have a ratings stronghold in the month-long span between June and July – a time when sports interest might be at its lowest.

Ultimately, under this schedule, the regular season would fall in an optimum time where the Association could take full advantage of traditional sports lulls, while avoiding interference with other sports (i.e. college basketball’s March Madness; NFL playoff races/playoffs; the NHL Playoffs). It gives monopoly to its own brand, without taking attention from other leagues – everybody wins.



In the realm of professional sport, the bottom line is more money. Under the proposed schedule push, I think it could provide just that – in the form of more fan interest and, subsequently, more fan revenue, for both players and owners. Considering this was one of the key issues in the NBA Lockout, I would think that it couldn’t hurt to look into something like this.

Just sayin'.
