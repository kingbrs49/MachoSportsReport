NFL 2006: Offensive Improvements Shown by 49ers

by Paulo C Camacho

October 18, 2006




So, the Niners lost again. It's really no huge surprise: when you go up against one of the best defenses in the league – while, at the same time, you have a sieve-like defense, yourself – the odds aren’t really in your favor. Philip Rivers looked like Peyton Manning, and LaDainian Tomlinson scored four – count 'em, FOUR – touchdowns on the ground. San Diego’s offense is what it is – and that is a great one, only looking to get better.

Let me say this – I'm not stating, by any means, San Fran should have beaten their neighbors from down south. All I'm saying is they showed improvement, even in a blowout. At least, they did on offense.

When you realize the firepower San Diego has on defense – Ranked No. 1, overall, by the way – you have to be encouraged by the job Alex Smith did against them. He led impressive drives down the field – with a battered offensive line, no less – against the best linebacker core in the league, led by sophomore sensation Shawne Merriman, coming at him the entire time. You could even say they were doing just about everything right, even on defense, up until the third quarter – sure, their secondary was subpar, but don’t forget – they were without Walt Harris, team leader on defense, as well as their best corner. Then, you look at the young defensive line getting to Rivers in the end zone for a safety, and you realize they got something going.

Granted, all the momentum they had from the safety was washed away with an INT by Luis Castillo on the ensuing possession, but, hey – the Chargers' D isn’t the best for nothing, right?

Look, even with the 48 points the Chargers scored on San Fran, in a blowout, you can take some good out of it, if you're a Niners fan. They gained 306 yards on offense against a team that gives up a league-best 214, all the while, scoring nineteen points – including a safety – against an early super bowl contender. Smith looks more and more poised with every game he starts. He knows how to use the weapons he has with the time he's given. And, don’t look now – Frank Gore didn’t fumble for the second game in a row. It's all encouraging.

Good offense; bad, yet young, defense. You take the bad with the good, but emphasize the good on an improving team.
