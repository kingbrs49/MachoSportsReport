Jon Dorenbos & An Examination of Long Snappers

by Paulo C Camacho

August 8, 2016




Thanks to a popular reality competition series, the position of NFL long snapper has been quietly thrown into the national spotlight.


Jon Dorenbos is a close-up card magician who had recently reached the semi-finals of “America’s Got Talent”, the long-running NBC talent competition. With stupefying tricks using everyday items like decks of playing cards, poster boards, and an average sketch book, Dorenbos has amazed judges Simon Cowell, Melanie Brown, Heidi Klum and Howie Mandel.


Case in point, this was his audition:

One of the main narratives on Dorenbos’ journey in "America’s Got Talent" (as is wont for a reality show to create) — aside from the unimaginable tragedy that befell his parents when he was 12 years old, and his lifelong passion for magic — was the “shocking revelation” that he was a 13-year veteran of the NFL.


It’s even in the video above, at 21 seconds in — Mandel seems to recognize his name, and correctly identifies him as a Philadelphia Eagle.


Now, you can easily blame this on TV reality production — a common device to frame a meaningful narrative — but, there’s one thing particularly suspicious about that part of the clip to me, as a sports fan, and especially as an NFL fan:


Do you really expect me to believe that Mandel actually knew who Jon Dorenbos was?


No offense to Dorenbos, or to his position. But, you have to admit — unless you’re a rabid, hardcore fan of a particular football team, one would be hard-pressed to readily identify the long snapper on any National Football League team.


I mean, seriously — do YOU know the long snapper’s name on your favorite team? I consider myself a loyal San Francisco 49ers fan, and I think that their long snapper is Kyle Nelson …


… turns out I was correct. Though, I had to look it up to be sure. But, I certainly wouldn’t be able to recognize him if I ran into him on the street — let alone at random on a national reality show.


My point is, the position of long snapper is probably one of the most anonymous in the sport of American Football.


In retrospect, it’s rather odd that it is — after all, long snapper is a pretty important, if not overlooked, position. He delivers the ball to the holder on field goal and extra point attempts; and he delivers the ball to the punter on punt plays. A good long snapper delivers the ball cleanly, with one or two 250-pound-plus defenders bearing down on him, every single time.


And the cost of bad snaps are pretty big, depending on the situation — from a botched field goal; to surrendering great field position to the opposing team; to an inevitable breakdown of special teams, altogether; it’s no wonder the long snapper is seen as a full-time specialty position. As it is, long snappers are so taken-for-granted, that the only time we, in the sports world, seem to talk about them, is when they screw up.


Just ask infamous Steelers LB James Harrison, who volunteered to take long snapping duties for injured regular long snapper Greg Warren. His only attempt sailed over punter Mitch Berger’s head, which led directly to 1) a game-tying safety for the New York Giants; and 2) the ensuing touchdown drive that won it for the G-Men.


Or ask Trey Junkin, who had two bad snaps that led to botched field goal attempts, in a 2003 wild-card game against the San Francisco 49ers — one of the greatest second-half comebacks in playoff history, that will forever be known for its insane finish:

Ultimately, long snappers have a tough job — one compounded by the fact that they make it look so easy. And, considering such a small margin for error, it’s a wonder that they’ve mastered such a skill, to begin with.


Kind of like a magic trick.


When you really think about it, if you compare what Dorenbos is currently doing on the main stage of “America’s Got Talent”, to what he’s been doing on NFL fields for the past 13 years, it’s not all that dissimilar. In both areas, Dorenbos has been honing his skills for decades, to the point where both feats look effortless.


The only difference? We fully expect him to snap the ball perfectly every time — after all, that’s his job.


But, we surely don’t expect him to make a drawing of the five of clubs float out of a sketch pad, only to reveal it had been drawn on the entire time.


It’s no secret why the position of long snapper isn’t as heralded as, say, the quarterback or the wide receiver. It’s not a glamorous position, and it’s involved in some of the most routine plays of the sport. But, don’t let its blandness fool you — it’s certainly an important position.


And, as Dorenbos’ journey in the reality series continues to unfold, it will be interesting to see what the Eagles end up doing with their self-professed “Magic Man” — both with a deck of cards, and a football, in his hands.


Something tells me that, no matter what, Jon Dorenbos is an invaluable part of the NFL. Just like the men who share his position in the league.
